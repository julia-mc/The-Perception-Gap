## Exploratory Data Analysis Summary

- Joined the dispatch dataset with the crime dataset to compare reported incidents with confirmed outcomes  
- Created variables to measure the perception gap, including whether a call resulted in a crime report and whether the severity matched  
- Built a severity ranking system (low, medium, high) for both reported and confirmed incidents  
- Aggregated the data by district and month to look at trends over time  
- Created visualizations to explore how the perception gap varies across districts  


### Key Variables

- **no_crime_report**: Shows whether a dispatch call resulted in a confirmed crime
- **mismatch**: Compares the initial reported incident type with the final crime classification
- **severity_shift**:
  - Severity Match -> perception was correct
  - Severity Downgraded -> people thought it was more serious than it actually was
  - Severity Upgraded -> people thought it was less serious than it actually was
  - N/A (No Crime) -> no crime was confirmed


### Key Findings

- Most dispatch calls do not result in a confirmed crime report.
- This means the perception gap is mainly driven by the large number of non-crime incidents, not just misclassification.
- When a crime is confirmed, the reported severity is often correct.
- Underestimation happens slightly more often than overestimation, although both occur at relatively low rates.
- The perception gap is high across all districts, with most showing no report rates around 80%–85%.
- Even though there are small differences between districts, the overall pattern is pretty consistent.


### Interpretation

Overall, the perception gap reflects differences between how people interpret situations and how police classify them.

The high number of non crime reports could mean that people are being cautious or reporting situations that seem suspicious but don’t meet the legal definition of a crime.


### Limitations

- The severity classification is based on keyword rules, so it may not capture every situation perfectly.
- Some cases are unclassified due to missing or unclear information.
- This is an exploratory analysis, so it doesn’t explain *why* these patterns happen yet.



### Next Steps

The next step is to run statistical models to better understand what might be driving the perception gap, including factors like citations, bias incidents, and call volume.
